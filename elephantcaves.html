About Elephanta Caves

The Elephanta Caves are a network of caves located on Elephanta Island, off the coast of Mumbai. These ancient rock-cut temples date back to the 5th to 8th centuries and are dedicated to the Hindu god Shiva. The caves are known for their impressive sculptures, including the famous Trimurti, a large sculpture of a three-headed Shiva.

The caves were declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987 and are one of the most important historical sites in India. They provide a glimpse into the rich cultural and artistic heritage of ancient India.

History

The Elephanta Caves were carved out of solid rock during the Gupta period and later expanded during the Kalachuri dynasty. The most famous feature of the caves is the Trimurti sculpture, which stands 6 meters tall and depicts Shiva in three forms: the creator, the preserver, and the destroyer.

The caves were rediscovered by the Portuguese in the 16th century, and since then, they have attracted scholars, artists, and tourists from around the world.
